Aftacare App Case Study

<> Project Overview </>

Enrol and pay for Aftercare from an app on your phone.

Aftacare is a service that enables online aftercare enrollment and payment. The typical user is between 40-45 years old, and most users are parents or guardians. Aftacare’s goal is to make aftercare enrollment and payments fun, fast and easy.

Date

Dec 2023 - Jan 2024

Role

This is an individual project that allowed me to plan and direct each step of the design thinking process as a UX design student with mobile and web UI design experience.

Responsibilities

Conduct user research, paper and digital wireframing, low and high-fidelity prototyping, conducting usability studies, accounting for accessibility, iterating on designs and responsive design.

The Problem

Enrollment into aftercares can be a cumbersome and time-consuming task for parents and guardians. They want a fast and easy way to find an aftercare, enroll their kids and make payments.

The Goal

Develop an aftercare platform that will function seamlessly across devices. This would replace the the cumbersome paper enrollment process, that leaves parents and guardians out of the loop, and cash payment processes with a secure and streamlined platform for online aftercare enrollment and payments.

<> Understanding The User </>

User Research / Personas / Problem Statements / User Journey Maps

<> User Research: Summary </>

I conducted primary research using a google survey to understand the challenges users experienced during the enrolment and payment process at aftercares. One of the assumptions that I had was that parents were frustrated with the paper enrolment process. After conducting research, it seems that it didn’t really bother them. I was also quite surprised by some of the feedback. I didn’t realize that what was frustrating parents was how early they have to enroll to get accepted.


Fees are expensive so the design needs to reward users, save money or make it more affordable.


Parents do not have the time to help their children with homework after work so the design needs to ensure children can be assisted with homework.


Parents do not know how much space is available and they apply early to ensure they get space so the design needs to ensure that parents know how much space is available and when to apply.


Parents do not know if they can trust the teachers to truly look after their kids at aftercare so the design needs to consider how teachers or the aftercare can be established as trustworthy.

<> User Persona </>

Problem Statement:

Damian is a full time working dad who needs to enrol his kids easily into aftercare because it’s difficult to look after his kids when he is away for work.

<> Starting The Design </>

Paper Wireframes / Digital Wireframes / Low-fidelity Prototype / Usability Studies

<> Paper Wireframes </>

After understanding research insights, creating information architecture, user flows and a competitive audit, it was time to put pen to paper. I brainstormed five wireframes, starred the parts I liked and created the first final version for each screen.

<> Digital Wireframes </>

After sketching all the wireframes, I started creating digital wireframes with placeholders for text and images. I made sure to include headings, icons and text that were important for navigation.

<> Digital Wireframes </>

Parents and guardians have busy lives because of work. My goal was to streamline the search process. I made sure to include navigation that made searching easy and fast whilst remaining informative enough to make a decision.

<> Lofi Prototype </>

To create a low-fidelity prototype, I connected all of the screens involved in the primary user flow of enrolling a child into an aftercare and paying.

<> Usability Study: Findings </>

After conducting usability studies, I applied the note-taking method, created affinity diagrams and identified themes to discover insights.

Study Type: Unmoderated Usability Study
Location: South Africa (remote)
Participants: 5 Participants
Length: 10 minutes

Round 1

Users should be able to view aftercare reviews during their search.

The home page’s look and navigation to be concise and less overwhelming.

Bigger buttons for easy and intended navigation without getting stuck.

Round 2

Users should be able to view the address of an aftercare.

During enrollment, users should be able to indicate whether their child needs meals daily.

During enrollment, users should be able to provide emergency contact info.

<> High-fidelity Prototype </>

The hi-fi prototype followed the same user flow as the lo-fi prototype, and included the design changes made after the usability study.

<> Accessability Considerations </>

I made sure the use of colour was accessible for people who are visually impaired and do not see the world as a normal person would. This means that they would easily be able to navigate the app.

I also considered the form fields input by including “*required” to clearly indicate which form fields are required. This also means people who are visually impaired do not have to rely on colour.

I ensured that motion was not too slow or fast between screens and interactions so that it wasn’t disturbing or distracting.

<> Takeaways </>

Takeaways / Next Steps

<> Impact: </>

“The app is great at streamlining the process of selecting an aftercare for children. I liked how easy it was to select an aftercare from the map, and how the aftercare allowed you to choose from a selection of activities for your child once your enrolled.”

<> What I learned: </>

I learnt that great impactful design ideas can come from anyone. It’s vital not to be biased. The most important takeaway for me is to always design for accessibility first.
